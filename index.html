Problem Statement

What We Set Out to Do

For our final project, we created a robot arm that is fully voice-controlled, integrating Robosuite with the Vosk API. To benchmark what voice control can do, we attempted two difficult Robosuite tasks: nut assembly and multi-cube stacking. We provide commands for dexterous control: low-level position commands such as “forward,” “back,” “up,” and “down”; waypoint-style commands such as “grab,” “hover,” and “place”; and highest-level commands like “stack” and “assemble” that can run an entire task. This problem framing was inspired by the sample multimodal-processing statement in the project rubric.

To achieve fine-grained control, we designed a layered command system spanning three levels of autonomy:

AUTO

Fully Autonomous

High-level commands like "stack" and "assemble" execute the entirety of a given task without any further input.

SEG

Segmented Autonomous

Mid-level waypoint commands like "grab", "hover", and "place" let the user guide the robot through individual phases.

INCR

Incremental Control

Low-level commands such as “up,” “down,” “left,” “right,” “forward,” “back,” “clockwise,” and “counterclockwise” apply short bursts of motion in world axes (see Methods).

Speech Recognition

We run Vosk locally on the CPU using a small English model (vosk-model-small-en-us-0.15). At about 40 MB and roughly 300 MB RAM, it runs alongside the MuJoCo simulation on a laptop.

Audio is captured with SoundDevice, read in ~0.25 s blocks at the device’s native rate. Each block is downmixed to mono, resampled to 16 kHz with polyphase resampling, peak-normalized when float samples exceed ±1, then converted to 16-bit PCM with clipping. Chunks are passed to Vosk’s KaldiRecognizer.

We call SetGrammar with a JSON list of allowed command strings so decoding is restricted to our vocabulary, which improves reliability for short keywords compared to open-ended dictation.

Command Routing

Recognized text is matched against keyword sets depending on context (e.g. wait for start, then stack / grab / assemble / incremental jog words, or hover / place in segmented motion). This selects which controller runs: incremental teleop, segmented grasp–hover–place, or a full autonomous stack / assemble sequence.

Incremental teleop: Commands like up, down, left, right, forward, back, clockwise, and counterclockwise do not set a Cartesian target relative to the current end-effector. They apply a fixed delta in world axes (e.g. +Z for “up”) of 0.048 m per control step, clipped to ±0.14 m per axis, repeated for 26 steps at the environment control rate (20 Hz in our scripts). Each spoken jog is a short burst of motion, intentionally discrete under speech latency.

Waypoint stack and nut tasks: For stack / assemble and the grab–hover–place workflow, policies use observations cubeA_pos and cubeB_pos as grasp vs. place (or support) objects. For multi-layer stacking we assign those fields per layer to the appropriate pair. A PID controller tracks named waypoints. Segmented runs pause between grab, hover, and place until the next keyword, using the same geometry as the fully autonomous run.

Control & Movement via PID

Once the robot knows its target, a PID controller drives the end-effector to that position. PID gives two key advantages over open-loop movement: it self-corrects if the robot deviates from course due to obstacles or physics errors, and it produces smooth acceleration rather than jerky steps.

Each simulation step, the controller outputs a 7-dimensional action vector: [ΔX, ΔY, ΔZ, axis-angle × 3, gripper]. The simulation runs at 20 Hz (dt = 0.05 s).

Parameter Value Rationale
Kp (Proportional) 10.0 High gain to reach target quickly within a small movement budget
Kd (Derivative) 5.0 Damps oscillation and prevents overshoot at the target position
Ki (Integral) 0.1 Small value to eliminate steady-state error without introducing drift

Evaluation

Results and Success Criteria

We defined six success criteria: for each of our two environments (multi-cube stacking and nuts & pegs), we aimed to succeed at greater than 80% using each of the three motion styles (fully autonomous, segmented, incremental). In stacking, success meant stacking at least three cubes; in nuts & pegs, successfully placing each nut on its peg. We ran 10 trials per case.

Demo highlight — ~30 s stack

Multi-Cube Stacking

Fully autonomous and segmented modes benefited from the same waypoint-style manipulation we built for earlier coursework: structured phases (hover, grasp, hover, place) are easier to tune and stabilize.

Incremental was hardest: speech latency and bursty jog commands mean the robot does not stream continuous motion; the user must correct repeatedly. Small timing differences matter most in contact-heavy phases. We still stacked three cubes in 9 of 10 incremental trials.

Demo highlight — ~51 s assembly

Nuts & Pegs

Nut assembly stresses precision and rotation. Incremental teleop reached 8 of 10 successes; the burst-style control and alignment demands of peg insertion made this mode the most variable.

Overall we met the 80% threshold for all six criteria. The limiting factor in incremental mode was less the grasp–place mechanics than the burst teleop interaction under recognition delay.

Discussion

Challenges & Lessons Learned

The hardest part of the project was integrating Vosk and managing latency. Our initial plan was continuous motion, updating plans only when the user spoke; in practice, the model often reacted on the order of ~2 seconds after a command, which is unacceptable for tight manipulation. We expect a much stronger GPU could cut inference latency and eventually support continuous action instead of discrete bursts; for now, discrete steps remain necessary.

Incremental Teleop Under Speech Delay

Because control arrives as short spoken steps, the system must map each recognized word to a small motion while tolerating misrecognitions and gaps between utterances. We tuned step size and clamped targets to stay precise and limit cumulative drift across repeated commands. We also kept behavior predictable when commands are late or out of order by avoiding runaway drift and using a consistent stop-and-wait cadence between jogs.

Reliable short-keyword recognition still depended on careful audio preprocessing and SetGrammar-style vocabulary restriction.

Discrete Bursts as the Practical Design

Each jog applies a fixed world-frame delta for a bounded number of steps at 20 Hz, then stops. That design trades throughput for controllability under latency and matches how users issue voice commands in practice.

Future Directions

Vision-Language Models

The most promising next step is inserting a vision-language model (VLM) between the speech-to-text output and the position planning stage. This would enable complex, natural language instructions instead of single keywords — commands like "grab the blue cube and put it on the red one" would become possible without hardcoded policies.

Full 6-DoF Control

We built on Assignment 1’s robot and environments: the arm currently has 4 of 6 degrees of freedom (no roll or pitch). Adding those axes would enable harder tasks such as opening a door or manipulating objects at arbitrary orientations.
